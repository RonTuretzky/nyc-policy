NYC Crypto Programs

Leftist Analysis & Strategic Hypotheticals

December 2025

Examining crypto through economic justice, public ownership, worker power, and democratic governance

Framework

Analyzing NYC crypto programs through leftist principles:

  • Economic Justice - Who benefits? Who bears risk?
  • Public Ownership - Public vs. private control
  • Worker Power - Labor representation in governance
  • Financial Inclusion - Rhetoric vs. reality
  • Democratic Governance - Community voice in decisions

Contradiction #1

Public-Private Partnership Model

Public Provides:

  • NYCEDC funding
  • City agency access
  • Regulatory cooperation
  • Legitimacy

Private Receives:

  • Operational control
  • Agenda-setting power
  • Profits
  • Socialized risk
"Accumulation by dispossession" — public goods converted into private profit opportunities

Contradiction #2

Advisory Council Composition

Who's at the Table:

  • Figure Technologies (fintech)
  • Traction & Scale (PE)
  • Galaxy Digital (crypto fund)
  • 150+ industry executives

Who's Excluded:

  • Labor unions
  • Tenant organizations
  • Immigrant rights groups
  • Consumer protection orgs
  • Public bank advocates

Council composition predetermines industry-favorable recommendations

Contradiction #3

Financial Inclusion: Rhetoric vs. Reality

Official claim: Crypto benefits "unbanked and underbanked communities"

Reality Check:

  • Crypto volatility = disproportionate risk for low-income users
  • 2022 crash wiped out wealth from retail investors (many people of color)
  • Literacy campaigns don't address root causes: poverty wages, predatory banking
  • No concrete wealth-building programs for marginalized communities
The solution isn't teaching poor people to speculate—it's public banking, living wages, rent control, and universal basic services.

Contradiction #4

Surveillance and Data Extraction

MyCity Portal & Digital Wallet:

  • Aggregates data from 4+ city agencies (including social services)
  • Plans to track food purchases for "healthier behavior"
  • CTO refused to confirm refusal of law enforcement data requests
  • Private contractors have access to sensitive information
This is welfare surveillance dressed up as technological innovation. Data collection is weaponized against poor people for fraud prosecutions, benefit denials, and immigration enforcement.

Contradictions #5 & #6

Remittances

Positive: Reducing Western Union extraction

But:

  • No immigration reform advocacy
  • Doesn't address root causes of migration
  • Workaround, not systemic change

BitBonds

Financialization of public infrastructure

  • Taxpayer exposure to Bitcoin volatility
  • 10% to speculative BTC reserve
  • Comptroller correctly rejected
"Cryptocurrencies are not sufficiently stable to finance the City's infrastructure, affordable housing, or schools."

Potential Leftist Utilities

Blockchain could serve leftist goals if fundamentally redirected:

Cooperatives

  • Worker coop governance
  • Community land trusts
  • Mutual aid networks
  • Time banking

Accountability

  • Campaign finance tracking
  • Police misconduct records
  • Budget transparency
  • Contract monitoring

Rights

  • Tenant-controlled rent records
  • Wage theft documentation
  • Portable gig worker benefits
  • Immigrant ID credentials

Current NYC Gap: None of these applications are prioritized

Hypothetical #1

Public Ownership of Blockchain Infrastructure

Current: NYC Blockchain Center operated by VC affiliates

Refactored:

  • Municipalize as public utility
  • Board: 2 city reps, 2 CUNY, 2 labor, 2 community, 1 public tech
  • Mission: Public interest, not private profit
  • All code open source and public domain
  • No proprietary vendor lock-in

Political Path: City Council legislation + Tech Workers Coalition + tenant org coalition + Comptroller audits

Hypothetical #2

Community-Controlled Digital Wallets

Current: City-controlled wallet potentially tracking purchases, sharing with police

Refactored:

  • Public benefit corporation with community governance
  • Data minimization: collect only what's necessary
  • Explicit ban on data sharing with law enforcement, ICE
  • No behavioral tracking or purchase "incentives"
  • User-controlled data portability and deletion
  • Oversight board with majority benefit recipients

Hypothetical #3

Worker-Owned Remittance Cooperative

Current: City promotes private crypto companies (Bitso, Strike)

Refactored:

  • Worker-owned cooperative for remittances
  • Owned by immigrant workers who use it
  • Revenue returns to members or funds community development
  • Technical infrastructure from NYC Node (CUNY)
  • Integration with Public Bank NYC campaign
  • Multilingual: Spanish, Chinese, Bengali, Haitian Creole

Coalition: Make the Road NY, DRUM, Adhikaar + Worker Coop Business Development Initiative

Hypothetical #4

Tenant Power Blockchain

Current: BigApps Real Estate challenge focused on "asset management"

Refactored: Tenant-controlled documentation system for:

  • Rent payment history (tenant-verified, not landlord-controlled)
  • Repair requests with timestamps and photos
  • Housing code violation records
  • Harassment documentation
  • Integrated with 311 + Housing Court
  • Landlord cannot alter or delete tenant records

Coalition: Crown Heights Tenant Union, Met Council on Housing + Right to Counsel

Alternative Governance Frameworks

Municipal Digital Commons

Treat blockchain as public utility. Board: 50% community, 30% agencies, 20% experts. Cost recovery only, no profit. Universal service obligation.

Solidarity Tech Cooperative

Federation of worker coops, CLTs, mutual aid networks. Platform coop model. One member, one vote. Revenue sharing.

Community Data Trust

Data governed by those who generate it. Fiduciary duty to residents. Consent-based sharing. Community veto over harmful uses.

Democratic Tech Assessment

Mandatory community impact assessment. Right to reject deployments. Sunset clauses requiring re-authorization.

Strategic Recommendations

For Progressive Elected Officials

  1. Reframe the Debate: "How can technology serve communities?" not "How can it help industry?"
  2. Demand Transparency: Expose funding flows, contract terms, decision-making
  3. Condition Public Investment: Require community governance, open source, privacy by design, labor standards, ICE data ban
  4. Support Alternatives: Fund cooperative and public infrastructure over private platforms
  5. Connect to Broader Agenda: Link crypto policy to housing, immigration, worker power, public banking

Strategic Recommendations

For Community Organizations & Labor

Community Orgs

  • Build technical capacity (Tech Workers Coalition, Data for Black Lives)
  • Demand seats at advisory tables
  • Document harms to marginalized communities
  • Develop alternative visions with sympathetic technologists

Labor Unions

  • Organize tech workers on city contracts
  • Negotiate over blockchain system deployment
  • Oppose surveillance of workers and benefit recipients
  • Advocate for worker ownership in public-supported ventures
