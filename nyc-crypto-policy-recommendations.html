What Exactly is Executive Order 57?

An Executive Order is a directive signed by the Mayor that creates policy, establishes offices, or directs city agencies to take action. It does NOT require City Council approval. It can be rescinded or replaced by the next mayor.

EO 57 Specifically Does Three Things:

  1. Creates a new office — the "Office of Digital Assets and Blockchain" — inside OTI (the city's tech agency)
  2. Appoints a director — Moises Rendon — who reports to CTO Matthew Fraser
  3. Assigns a mission: "promote the responsible use of digital assets," "foster the crypto ecosystem," "attract world-class talent," and "reinforce the city's standing as the world's hub for financial and technological innovation"

The Problem:

The mission is primarily industry promotion ("foster ecosystem," "attract talent," "world's hub") rather than resident protection. It's essentially a crypto recruitment office disguised as public service.

What Real Crypto Public Education Should Include

Instead of industry promotion, the city should provide honest, balanced education to help residents make informed decisions:

Legitimate Uses

  • Remittances: Stablecoins (USDC, USDT) can save 90%+ on fees to Latin America, Caribbean
  • Inflation protection: For immigrants from high-inflation countries (Argentina, Venezuela)
  • Banking alternative: Those rejected by traditional banks can hold stablecoins
  • Cross-border payments: Faster, cheaper than wire transfers

Serious Risks

  • Volatility: Bitcoin can drop 50%+ overnight — not a savings account
  • Scams: $1.4B lost to crypto scams in 2024; 10% of all financial fraud
  • No FDIC protection: If exchange fails, money is gone
  • Complexity: Wallets, keys, addresses — mistakes are irreversible

Who It's Not For

  • Emergency savings (use FDIC-insured bank)
  • Rent money or essential expenses
  • Anyone who can't afford to lose it
  • Anyone without time to learn the technology
  • Get-rich-quick seekers
This is what a repurposed office could do: Partner with DCWP Financial Empowerment Centers to provide balanced crypto education — including when NOT to use it — at the 40+ centers across the city.

Crypto Tax Payments: The Stablecoin Solution

Problems with Volatile Crypto (BTC, ETH)

  • Capital gains tax: Paying with appreciated crypto triggers a taxable event for the payer
  • HODL mentality: With BTC up 125% in 2024, holders don't want to spend
  • Adoption is low: Colorado saw <80 payments in 3 years (<$60K total)
  • Volatility: Price can swing during transaction

Stablecoins Solve These Problems

  • No volatility: USDC, USDT, PayPal USD pegged 1:1 to dollar
  • No capital gains: No appreciation = no tax event when spending
  • Already used: PayPal USD supported in Detroit; Bitso uses stablecoins for remittances
  • Instant conversion: City receives USD, never holds crypto
<80
Total crypto tax payments in Colorado over 3 years (2022-2024)
Total amount: less than $60,000

Crypto Tax Payments: Recommendation

Nuanced View: This isn't inherently a bad idea IF implemented correctly. The key is design choices — stablecoins, instant conversion, and whether it serves a real need.

If NYC Explores This, Require:

1. Stablecoin support — USDC, USDT, or PayPal USD to avoid volatility and capital gains issues
2. Instant USD conversion — city never holds crypto, receives dollars only
3. Competitive vendor selection — RFP process, not sole-source to crypto industry friends
4. Cost-benefit analysis — is there actual demand? Colorado's experience suggests minimal adoption
5. Don't prioritize — this is a convenience feature for a small group, not a public benefit priority

Key Question:

With <80 payments in 3 years in Colorado, is this worth the implementation cost? Probably low priority unless there's evidence of actual NYC demand.

BitBonds: The Progressive Case (And Why It Fails)

The Tempting Argument

  • If NYC invested $100M in Bitcoin 5 years ago, it would be worth ~$1.35B today
  • Those gains could fund:
    • Rent control enforcement
    • Affordable housing construction
    • NYCHA repairs
    • Tenant legal assistance
  • Wall Street uses speculative strategies — why can't the city?
  • States (New Hampshire, Arizona) are considering reserves

Why Progressives Should Reject It

  • Asymmetric risk: Gains benefit the budget, but losses hit taxpayers and services
  • Moral hazard: Encourages speculative mindset in government
  • Opportunity cost: What if the money had gone to housing directly 5 years ago?
  • Volatility: Can't budget on assets that swing 50% in months
  • Legal barriers: Tax-exempt bond arbitrage rules, no NYC mechanism
  • IMF lesson: El Salvador's Bitcoin bet blocked emergency lending

BitBonds: Could It Fund Rent Control Relief?

The Question: If NYC had a Bitcoin reserve that appreciated, could gains fund progressive programs like rent control enforcement, tenant legal aid, or affordable housing?

Theoretical Scenario

  • NYC issues $1B in BitBonds
  • $100M (10%) used to buy Bitcoin
  • Bitcoin doubles in 5 years: $100M → $200M
  • City's 50% share of gains: $50M
  • Could fund: ~1,000 affordable units OR 5 years of expanded tenant legal services

Downside Scenario

  • NYC issues $1B in BitBonds
  • $100M (10%) used to buy Bitcoin
  • Bitcoin crashes 60% (as in 2022): $100M → $40M
  • City still owes bondholders $1B + interest
  • $60M loss comes from... budget cuts?
  • Same services threatened: housing, tenant aid
Bottom Line: The potential gains don't justify the risk to public funds. If the goal is funding rent control relief, the honest answer is: raise progressive taxes, don't gamble on crypto. The city shouldn't run a hedge fund.

If a future administration considers a crypto reserve despite our recommendation, these guardrails would reduce harm:

Final Recommendation: Even with guardrails, the core problem remains — public funds shouldn't be subject to speculative asset volatility. Keep BitBonds rejected. If NYC wants more housing revenue, pursue mansion taxes, pied-à-terre taxes, and commercial rent taxes — not Bitcoin gambling.

Framework for Future Tech Proposals

Questions to Ask About Any New Technology Initiative

  • Who benefits? Residents or industry? Low-income New Yorkers or tech investors?
  • Does the technology solve a real problem? Or is this a solution looking for a problem?
  • Would conventional technology work just as well? Blockchain is rarely the best tool.
  • What are the privacy implications? Who has access to data? Can it be shared with police or ICE?
  • Is procurement transparent? Competitive bidding, public contracts, Council oversight?
  • Who has a seat at the table? Just industry, or also labor, tenants, consumers?
  • What's the opportunity cost? Could these funds better serve residents another way?
