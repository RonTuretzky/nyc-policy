# NYC Blockchain Plan: Deep Dive

## Overview

The **NYC Blockchain Plan** was released in December 2025 by the Office of Technology and Innovation (OTI). It represents the city's most comprehensive framework for evaluating and implementing blockchain technology across municipal services.

Unlike the Adams administration's other crypto initiatives—which were largely industry-promotion efforts—the Blockchain Plan takes a more measured approach, acknowledging that blockchain is "not one-size-fits-all" and must meet privacy and climate standards.

**First Pilot**: Asbestos certification with the Department of Environmental Protection (DEP)

**Global Use Cases Reviewed**: 100+

---

## What the Plan Proposes

### Proposed Use Cases

| Use Case | Description | Status |
|----------|-------------|--------|
| **Asbestos Certifications** | Digital verification of asbestos abatement compliance | First pilot (active) |
| **Vital Records** | Birth certificates, death certificates on blockchain | Under consideration |
| **Property Deeds** | Land registry and deed transfer verification | Under consideration |
| **City Payments** | Cryptocurrency acceptance for taxes/fees | Low priority |

### Plan Positives

- Reviewed 100+ global blockchain implementations before proposing pilots
- Acknowledges blockchain isn't appropriate for all use cases
- Requires alignment with privacy and climate standards
- More deliberate than other Adams crypto initiatives

### Plan Concerns

- Limited community input in development
- Industry-heavy consultation process
- Unclear cost-benefit analysis requirements
- Privacy implications not fully addressed

## Crypto-Left Use Cases: Repurposing the Blockchain Plan

The infrastructure and expertise being developed for the NYC Blockchain Plan can be redirected toward community-benefit use cases. Here's how:

### 1. Participatory Budgeting on Blockchain

**Current NYC PB**: ~$40M allocated annually across 35 council districts. Paper/web voting with limited accessibility.

**Blockchain-Enhanced PB**:
- **Quadratic voting**: Prevents wealthy stakeholders from dominating, amplifies broad consensus
- **Transparent allocation**: All votes and fund distributions recorded on-chain for public audit
- **Delegation**: Residents can delegate votes to trusted community representatives
- **Low-cost participation**: Use Snapshot-style off-chain voting with on-chain verification

**Precedents**:
- [Moscow Active Citizen](https://www.mos.ru/en/news/item/80669073/): 5M+ users, blockchain-verified voting since 2017
- [Barcelona Decidim](https://decidim.org/): Open-source platform used by 150+ cities globally
- [Zug, Switzerland](https://www.stadtzug.ch/blockchain): First blockchain municipal vote (2018)

### 2. Community Land Trust Records

**Problem**: CLT property records scattered across multiple agencies; transfers are slow and expensive.

**Blockchain Solution**:
- Immutable record of CLT deed restrictions
- Automated enforcement of resale price limits
- Transparent ownership history
- Reduced title insurance costs

**Why It Works for Crypto-Left**:
- Protects permanently affordable housing
- Prevents deed restriction violations
- Community-controlled governance
- Aligns with housing justice goals

### 3. Worker Cooperative Governance

**Use Cases**:
- **Membership tokens**: Represent stake in cooperative
- **Democratic voting**: One member, one vote (not token-weighted)
- **Profit distribution**: Automated, transparent surplus allocation
- **Record keeping**: Immutable meeting minutes and decisions

**NYC Context**:
- 150+ worker cooperatives in NYC
- Many lack affordable governance infrastructure
- Could integrate with [Worker Cooperative Business Development Initiative](https://www.nyc.gov/site/sbs/businesses/worker-cooperatives.page)

### 4. Mutual Aid Network Infrastructure

**Current State**: NYC mutual aid networks use fragmented tools (Google Sheets, Signal, Venmo)

**Blockchain-Enhanced Mutual Aid**:
- **Transparent fund management**: All contributions and distributions visible
- **Automated matching**: Smart contracts match needs with resources
- **Privacy-preserving**: Zero-knowledge proofs for sensitive requests
- **Interoperability**: Networks can share resources without centralized control

**Precedents**:
- [Gitcoin Grants](https://gitcoin.co/grants/): Quadratic funding for public goods
- [Coordinape](https://coordinape.com/): Peer-to-peer compensation allocation

### 5. NYC Community Currency

**Concept**: Local currency that circulates within NYC neighborhoods, keeping economic value local.

**Blockchain Implementation**:
- **Stablecoin-backed**: Pegged 1:1 to USD, no volatility
- **Programmable incentives**: Bonus tokens for shopping at local businesses
- **EBT integration**: Seamless use with benefits
- **Demurrage option**: Small holding fee encourages circulation

**Precedents**:
- [BerkShares](https://centerforneweconomics.org/apply/local-currencies-program/): Massachusetts local currency
- [Bristol Pound](https://bristolpound.org/): UK local currency (now digital)
- [Grassroots Economics](https://www.grassrootseconomics.org/): Kenya community currency network

### 6. Public Housing Maintenance Tracking

**Problem**: NYCHA maintenance requests often lost or delayed; residents lack visibility into status.

**Blockchain Solution**:
- Immutable record of maintenance requests and completions
- Resident verification of work done
- Transparent contractor performance data
- Automated escalation for overdue requests

**Why It Works**:
- Residents control their own records
- Accountability without surveillance
- Data can support advocacy efforts
- Integrates with existing 311 infrastructure

## Evaluation Framework for Crypto-Left Use Cases

Before implementing any blockchain pilot, require answers to:

### 1. Necessity Test
- Does blockchain solve a problem that conventional databases cannot?
- Is immutability actually needed, or is it a liability?
- Would a centralized solution work just as well?

### 2. Community Benefit Test
- Who primarily benefits—residents or industry?
- Was the community consulted in design?
- Are there labor, tenant, or consumer advocates involved?

### 3. Privacy Test
- What data is stored on-chain vs. off-chain?
- Can individuals be identified from on-chain data?
- Who controls access to personal information?
- Is there a right to be forgotten?

### 4. Cost-Benefit Test
- What is the total cost of implementation?
- What is the cost of alternatives?
- What are the ongoing maintenance costs?
- Who bears the costs if it fails?

### 5. Governance Test
- Who controls upgrades and changes?
- Is there community representation in governance?
- How are disputes resolved?
- What happens if the project is abandoned?

## Recommendations

### Day 1 Actions

1. **Pause vital records pilot** pending comprehensive privacy review
2. **Establish community advisory board** for all blockchain initiatives
3. **Require cost-benefit analysis** before any pilot proceeds
4. **Redirect asbestos pilot resources** if evaluation fails necessity test

### Short-Term (0-6 months)

1. **Pilot participatory budgeting enhancement** in one council district
2. **Partner with Greenpill NYC** for community use case development
3. **Publish all Blockchain Plan documentation** for public review
4. **Create community input process** for proposed pilots

### Medium-Term (6-18 months)

1. **Community Land Trust pilot** with HPD and community land trusts
2. **Worker cooperative governance tools** with SBS Worker Coop initiative
3. **Mutual aid infrastructure** partnership with existing networks
4. **NYC Community Currency feasibility study**

### Long-Term (18+ months)

1. **Full participatory budgeting rollout** citywide
2. **NYCHA maintenance tracking** pilot
3. **Integration with MyCity payment rails** (if privacy requirements met)
4. **Regional community currency network**
