Municipal Mesh WiFi PR #12

Cross-Reference Analysis

The NYC Internet Master Plan is the City's official broadband strategy document. PR #12 proposes Municipal Mesh WiFi as Proposal 3 for Decentral Park NYC's crypto policy recommendations to the Mamdani administration.

This slideshow identifies 10 areas of direct cross-reference between the IMP and the PR, plus statistics from the IMP that could strengthen the PR's arguments.

1. The Digital Divide: Shared Foundation

Both documents center the same core problem

IMP What the Master Plan says

  • 29% of NYC households lack a broadband subscription at home (p.10)
  • 1.5 million New Yorkers have neither mobile nor home broadband (p.10)
  • 40% of NYC City households lack combined home + mobile broadband (p.9, Executive Summary)
  • The Bronx has the lowest broadband adoption of any borough (p.13, p.32)
  • Neighborhoods with competitive broadband markets have higher household incomes (p.13)

PR #12 What the PR claims

  • 69% of NYC residents have only 1-2 ISP choices (citing NYC Broadband Report 2018)
  • 36.7% Bronx disconnection rate (citing NY State Comptroller 2024)
  • 7x income disparity between connected/disconnected households (citing IMP 2020)
  • $79.83/mo Bronx vs $56 Manhattan pricing gap

Direct citation The PR already cites the IMP for the 7x income disparity figure. The IMP's 29% / 1.5M / 40% stats could further bolster the PR.

2. ISP Competition & Lack of Choice

IMP Residential Broadband Choice (p.35)

  • Map 6 shows vast areas of the Bronx, Brooklyn, Queens with only 0-1 ISPs
  • Areas with 3+ providers are limited to wealthy, dense neighborhoods (p.35)
  • Households with 3+ providers have average incomes 50% greater than those with only 2 (p.35)
  • Commercial fiber choice is concentrated in Manhattan; outer boroughs have low/medium-low (p.38)

PR #12 Competition Claims

  • 69% of residents have 1-2 ISP choices
  • Proposes mesh WiFi as a community-owned alternative to break ISP monopoly
  • Cites Harvard Berkman Klein study on municipal network pricing being 25-50% lower

Usable stat IMP's Map 6 data + the "50% greater income" stat for areas with competition could be cited in the PR to show the equity case is the City's own finding.

4. NYCHA Rooftops & City Real Estate Assets

IMP City Assets Strategy

  • The IMP calls for "Optimize Public Assets" as a core implementation pillar (p.72)
  • City owns ~24,000 rooftops and poles suitable for broadband deployment (p.64)
  • Map 16 maps rooftop fixed wireless access opportunities by presence of City buildings (p.63)
  • The City established the Broadband City Asset Task Force (BCAT) to inventory all assets for broadband (p.19)
  • ConnectHome brought broadband to NYCHA in the Bronx via partnership with HUD and T-Mobile (p.49)
  • Figure 26 lists 180 Neighborhood Tabulation Areas with rooftops, rooms, and poles available (p.64)

PR #12 Day 1 Action

  • "Open NYCHA rooftops to community networks" - zero cost, unlocks 335 developments
  • Mayoral directive, no legislation needed
  • 50 public building supernodes for $50k total, reaching 25k residents at $2/household

Key stat for PR The IMP already inventoried 24,000 city-owned rooftops. The PR's NYCHA rooftop proposal is a subset of what the City's own plan already mapped and endorsed.

5. Community Networks: Red Hook & NYC Mesh

IMP Official Recognition (p.49)

  • IMP explicitly highlights resilient Wi-Fi networks operated by community-based organizations (CBOs)
  • Names Red Hook WiFi (launched after Hurricane Sandy via New America Foundation) as a model
  • References NYCEDC supporting community WiFi development in six neighborhoods
  • The ConnectBIZ program delivered broadband to industrial business zones
  • Calls for "Digital Stewards" - community members trained to install/maintain networks
  • Section on Mesh Wi-Fi (p.59): "Mesh Wi-Fi can be a way to share an internet connection and reduce the cost per user"

PR #12 Community Network Proposals

  • Stabilize Red Hook WiFi ($36k/yr)
  • Fund NYC Mesh ($2M grants)
  • Expand Digital Stewards program
  • NYC Mesh: $290 standard install, $300/node/year operating cost

Strong alignment The IMP already validates community mesh as a legitimate broadband strategy. The PR can frame its proposals as fulfilling the IMP's own vision.

6. LinkNYC & Franchise Agreements

IMP Franchise Framework (p.46)

  • Figure 20 shows franchise agreement timeline: Cable (2021), Information Services (2022), Public Communications (2026)
  • LinkNYC described as the "largest, fastest municipal WiFi system in the world" with 5M+ users (p.49)
  • IMP notes franchise agreements can last up to 15 years and are periodically renegotiated (p.46)
  • DOITT is empowered to negotiate and enter into telecommunications franchises (p.47)

PR #12 LinkNYC Critique

  • CityBridge owes $73M+ to the City (DiNapoli audit 2021)
  • LinkNYC missed 41% of kiosk installation targets
  • Calls for renegotiation of LinkNYC agreement
  • Year 1 priority: redirect LinkNYC money to community infrastructure

Opportunity IMP's 2026 franchise renewal window for "Public Communications" is now. The PR could cite this timeline as the legal mechanism for renegotiation.

8. Open Access Infrastructure Model

IMP Network Architecture (p.56, p.64)

  • IMP calls for open access fiber optic infrastructure to every street intersection (p.12)
  • Estimated cost: $2.1 billion for citywide open access fiber (p.12)
  • Neighborhood Fiber Collocation Facilities (NFCFs) - one per 180 neighborhoods (p.63)
  • Each NFCF connects 120-150 assets per neighborhood
  • Supports multiple operators on shared, neutral infrastructure
  • Streetlight poles identified as key assets for wireless equipment (p.43)
  • IMP's "neutral host wireless" vision: shared spectrum network throughout every neighborhood (p.56)

PR #12 Mesh Network Proposal

  • Deploy 50 public building supernodes ($50k / 25k residents)
  • Expedite streetlight pole permits for community broadband
  • Community-owned, decentralized model vs. incumbent monopoly
  • Mesh is 40x more cost-effective than incumbent subsidies

Alignment The PR's supernode model maps directly to the IMP's NFCF concept. The IMP already endorsed shared, neutral infrastructure and streetlight-based wireless.

10. Implementation Phases: Unfulfilled Promises

IMP 4-Phase Plan (p.72-78)

  • Phase 1: Introduce New Role for City Government - Universal Solicitation for Broadband (USB)
  • Phase 2: Introduce New City role via public-private partnerships, open access infrastructure
  • Phase 3: Expand Service to More New Yorkers - iterate on public fiber, expand to underserved areas
  • Phase 4: Ensure All New Yorkers Benefit from Connectivity - digital inclusion, digital rights
  • Timeline: 2020-2025 for initial phases

GAP What Didn't Happen

  • The IMP was published in January 2020 - COVID hit 2 months later
  • Instead of open access fiber, the City launched Big Apple Connect - $90M to incumbents (Spectrum/Optimum)
  • No Universal Solicitation for Broadband was ever issued
  • NYCHA rooftops were never systematically opened to community networks
  • The PR's proposals fill the gap between the IMP's vision and what was actually implemented

Framing opportunity The PR can position its mesh WiFi proposal as completing the IMP's unfinished work - using the City's own blueprint.

Recommended PR Enhancements

Actions based on this cross-reference analysis

  • 1. Cite the IMP as official precedent. The PR's mesh WiFi proposal aligns with the IMP's own vision for open access, neutral host wireless, and community networks. Frame it as "completing the City's 2020 plan."
  • 2. Add the $142B / 165K jobs economic impact. These are the City's own projections for universal broadband. They dwarf Chattanooga's case study in persuasiveness.
  • 3. Reference IMP Figure 16. NYC Mesh is already listed as a $0/month provider in the City's official pricing table. This is powerful validation.
  • 4. Cite the 24,000 rooftop inventory. The IMP already mapped city-owned rooftops for broadband. The NYCHA rooftop proposal isn't radical - it's using assets the City already cataloged.
  • 5. Use the 2026 franchise renewal window. The IMP's Figure 20 shows Public Communications franchises renew in 2026. LinkNYC renegotiation has a legal mechanism right now.
  • 6. Add the 650,000 household figure. The IMP projects 650K non-subscriber households could gain broadband through competition. This is the addressable market for mesh.
  • 7. Highlight the IMP's Mesh Wi-Fi section. Page 59 explicitly endorses mesh as reducing cost per user and enabling community ownership. This isn't a fringe idea - it's in the City's plan.
