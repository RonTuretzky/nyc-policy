**Existing mesh networks in NYC, focusing on NYC Mesh and Red Hook WiFi, are small-scale, community-driven efforts primarily using unlicensed Wi-Fi (2.4/5 GHz) mesh topologies with rooftop nodes for line-of-sight connectivity.**

- **NYC Mesh** (volunteer-run, started ~~2014, nycmesh.net): Hundreds of nodes serving 500+ households (with 1,500–3,000 new node requests annually, but growth limited by volunteers). Covers parts of Brooklyn, Manhattan, Queens, Bronx via mesh \+ some supernodes with better backhaul. Users buy equipment (~~$240 for routers/antennas) \+ \~$50 install (sliding scale/subsidies available); pay-what-you-can monthly donation for ops/expansion. No large disclosed annual budget—primarily donations/volunteer labor; some fiber/IXP uplinks at key nodes. Low overhead but reliant on community contributions.  
- **Red Hook WiFi** (project of Red Hook Initiative non-profit, started 2011–2012 post-Sandy): Peak \~20 nodes/hotspots (now \~10 active due to degradation, closures, interference); free public/outdoor WiFi in Red Hook, Brooklyn (limited indoor/public housing expansion due to NYCHA policies). Initial low-cost deployment (\<$300/node, \~$50–85 routers); major grants funded expansion ($1M Rise:NYC 2015 for \~20 nodes/solar; $1M HUD 2018; $125k NYC Complete Count). Ongoing: partner (Sky Packets) covers \~$3,000/month data/power; trained "Digital Stewards" (youth) for maintenance.

**Costs overall are low per node/user initially (equipment dominant for NYC Mesh; grants for Red Hook expansions) but ongoing uplink/backhaul, power, maintenance add up—especially without dedicated revenue.** No comprehensive public annual budgets found; operations lean/grassroots. Uplink often donated, shared fiber, or commercial (Verizon early for Red Hook).

**Organizational risks/efficiency**: Highly efficient/low-overhead (volunteers, non-profits) with strong community buy-in and resilience (e.g., Red Hook during Sandy; NYC Mesh COVID support). But scalability limited by volunteers (burnout, skill gaps), line-of-sight requirements, interference in dense urban areas, equipment degradation, funding intermittency, maintenance/logistics challenges, and power dependency (outages knock out nodes). Not efficient for citywide scale without professionalization.

**Recommendation**: Fund and integrate these organizations as community access layer/partners rather than duplicating—provide municipal backhaul (fiber/conduits), rooftop access on city property, training/grants for sustainability, and technical support. This leverages existing trust/expertise in underserved areas while building a municipal core (e.g., supernodes, IXP access) for scale/resilience. Avoid purely top-down to align with DSA/community-control values.

**Existing providers**: Dominant fixed broadband are Verizon (FiOS fiber), Spectrum (Charter cable), Optimum/Altice (cable). Fixed wireless (T-Mobile, Verizon 5G Home) and limited others (AT\&T in spots) supplement, but wired choice is often 1–2 per location/block.

**Limited choice/monopoly**: Many NYC areas/blocks have duopoly or effective monopoly for high-speed fixed broadband (FCC data shows high overall coverage but concentrated providers; low-income/public housing often fewer reliable options). Precise % varies by source/FCC BDC updates (as of recent data: significant portions with 1–2 providers; e.g., some metros/neighborhoods near-monopoly; statewide NY relatively better but NYC pockets poor). Digital divide persists: higher unconnected/low-adoption in low-income, communities of color, outer boroughs/public housing (\~19% citywide unconnected in older data; ACP enrolled \~1M but gaps remain).

**Pricing "rent" and comparison**: NYC prices often $50–100+/month (introductory low for 300–1Gbps, then higher; avg around US median \~$70–80 with equipment). Less competitive areas see 35%+ higher effective prices or fewer deals. Contrast apples-to-apples (similar speeds/plans): more competitive metros (e.g., Chicago often cited for better value/penetration/lower effective $/Mbps; some with municipal fiber or more fiber overbuilders show lower prices or faster tiers for similar cost). NYC density raises some infrastructure costs, but limited choice enables premium pricing vs. cities with 3+ wired options or public options.

**Other advantages of decentralized/mesh Internet in NYC**:

- Disaster resilience (Sandy proved Red Hook value; power/infrastructure outages).  
- Equity/digital divide closure in high-need areas (public housing, low-income, outer boroughs) via low/no-cost access \+ training.  
- Community control/governance, potential privacy (local routing), innovation (e.g., integrated sensors).  
- Lower long-term costs (user equipment vs. recurring ISP rents), redundancy to corporate monopolies.  
- Rapid deployment in dense urban (rooftops) vs. trenching fiber everywhere.

**Infrastructure mayor can contribute**:

- Access to public property (rooftops of schools/libraries/housing/authority buildings, parks, streetlights/poles—with permitting).  
- Existing city fiber/dark fiber, conduits, or IXP partnerships for backhaul.  
- Power/electrical access at city sites.  
- Streamlined zoning/permitting for antennas/nodes; rights-of-way use.  
- Partnerships with NYCHA, DOE, libraries for siting/training/adoption.  
- Budget/grant leverage (BEAD, state/federal digital equity, capital plan) for core infrastructure.

**What you might be missing/what I contribute**:

- **Backhaul is key bottleneck**—mesh excels at last-mile distribution but needs reliable, affordable high-capacity uplink (municipal fiber/wholesale critical; unlicensed spectrum crowded/interference-prone in NYC—consider WiFi6/7, better protocols, or licensed options).  
- **Scalability/maintenance**: Volunteer models don't scale citywide without hybrid professional/community staffing, liability insurance, monitoring tools, e-waste/power management plans.  
- **Regulatory/legal**: FCC rules on unlicensed bands/power; potential ISP pushback/lobbying; data privacy/security (decentralized pros/cons); integration with 5G/small cells.  
- **Funding/sustainability**: Beyond grants—ISP franchise fees/taxes, municipal broadband authority/bonds, user subsidies/sliding scales, revenue from premium services/sensors. Phased rollout (pilot high-need neighborhoods integrating existing meshes) with metrics (adoption, uptime, speeds, equity).  
- **Broader ecosystem**: Digital literacy/training (expand stewards model), device subsidies, evaluation vs. alternatives (fiber overbuilds, fixed wireless). Environmental impact (energy use). Political alignment: frame as public ownership/anti-monopoly, community empowerment—engage DSA-aligned groups, housing orgs, unions early.  
- **Risks/opportunities**: Cyber/resilience audits; hybrid model (municipal core \+ community mesh edges) for best of both.
