Introduction

About Decentral Park NYC

A collective exploring alternatives to capitalism through community organizing, cooperative economics, and radical imagination.

Our Mission

We believe technology should be governed democratically, with community input at every level. Our work focuses on alternatives to extractive digital infrastructure—mesh networks instead of corporate ISPs, community-controlled data instead of surveillance capitalism, participatory budgeting instead of opaque procurement.

Our Approach

We are acutely aware of long-standing risks and problems inherent in centralized and extractive digital infrastructure-building. This document provides both a critique of the Adams administration's crypto-centric approach and a set of alternative proposals grounded in genuine public benefit.

"Rest, and radical imagination. Creating space for all the ways we can build a better world together."

Guiding Framework

Our Core Principles

Every recommendation in this document is guided by these questions:

👥

Who Benefits?

Residents or industry? Low-income New Yorkers or tech investors? Programs should serve the public.

🎯

Does It Solve a Real Problem?

Or is this a solution looking for a problem? Technology for its own sake wastes public resources.

⚖️

Would Simpler Tech Work?

Blockchain is rarely the best tool. Before adding complexity, ask if conventional tech would serve just as well.

🔒

What Are the Privacy Risks?

Who has access to data? Can it be shared with police or ICE? Surveillance must be prevented by design.

🗳️

Who Has a Seat at the Table?

Just industry, or also labor, tenants, consumers? Democratic participation must be structural.

💰

What's the Opportunity Cost?

Could these funds better serve residents another way? Every dollar on industry promotion is a dollar not spent on housing.

Program 1

Office of Digital Assets & Blockchain Reduce Budget

Created October 14, 2025 via Executive Order 57. Primary recommendation: reduce the budget significantly and redirect remaining resources toward community use cases rather than industry promotion. Est. budget: $736K - $1.79M annually.

Budget Methodology

Estimate based on comparable NYC mayoral office staffing: Executive Director ($180-220K), 2 Policy Analysts ($140-180K), Admin ($65-85K), plus 35% benefits. Operating costs include office space within OTI, events/outreach, and consulting. Benchmarked against Mayor's Office of Climate & Media offices.

Sources: OTI FY25 Budget, NYC Payroll Data, Executive Order 57

Current Problems

  • Mission focused on industry promotion not residents
  • Emphasis on "world's hub" and "attract talent"
  • High-finance use cases prioritized

Suggested New Missions (if retained)

  • Community currencies for local economies
  • Mutual aid tools for neighborhood resilience
  • Cooperative infrastructure for worker ownership
  • Financial inclusion for unbanked residents

Recommended Path

Reduce budget significantly — cut industry promotion activities and redirect remaining resources toward community-focused initiatives. Amend EO 57 to shift from high-finance speculation to social utility. Partner with community organizations, not Wall Street.

Day 1 Actions

  • Reduce budget significantly — cut industry promotion spending
  • Amend EO 57 — rewrite mission statement
Program 2

NYC Crypto Advisory Council Reform

Currently 150+ industry executives with zero community representatives. Reform to include crypto-progressive voices and community stakeholders. Est. budget: $90K - $340K annually.

Budget Methodology

Advisory councils have low direct costs (unpaid members) but require staff support. Estimate: Council Coordinator (0.5-1 FTE: $45-110K), event costs ($15-75K), meeting expenses (4-6/year: $10-40K), communications/legal. Benchmarked against comparable NYC industry advisory bodies.

Sources: CoinDesk Advisory Council, NYC Tech Talent Pipeline

Current Problems

  • 150+ executives, zero community reps: No labor, tenant, or consumer advocates
  • High-finance focus: Emphasis on speculation over social utility
  • No transparency: No published membership, no meeting minutes

New Membership Structure

  • 1/3 crypto-progressive advocates: Regen, public goods, mutual aid builders
  • 1/3 community organizations: Labor, tenants, consumer groups, cooperatives
  • 1/3 industry (reformed): Focus on social impact, not speculation

Day 1 Action

Issue executive order reforming council membership. Require 2/3 non-industry representation. Involve local NYC community-based crypto collectives, worker cooperatives, tenant organizations, and consumer advocates instead of just industry people. Publish all meeting minutes. Shift focus from industry promotion to community benefit.

Program 4

NYC Node Program Maintain

Ethereum node at CUNY Queens College with educational curriculum. The best of Adams' crypto initiatives—low cost, public institution, actual workforce development. Est. budget: $74K - $232K annually.

Budget Methodology

Well-documented Ethereum validator costs: hardware ($5-15K one-time), electricity ($2-5K/yr). CUNY personnel: faculty coordinator (0.25-0.5 FTE: $25-60K), technical support ($15-40K), curriculum development ($10-30K), student stipends ($10-50K). Most cost-effective program in the portfolio.

Sources: NYCEDC NYC Node, Ethereum Staking, CUNY Salary Schedules

What It Does

  • Runs Ethereum validator node at CUNY
  • Provides hands-on blockchain education
  • Trains students in distributed systems
  • Public institution (not private operators)

Why It Works

  • Low cost — minimal ongoing expenses
  • Educational value — practical skills training
  • Public benefit — CUNY serves working-class students
  • No industry capture — no private operators

Recommendation

Maintain the program but review curriculum for industry bias. Add required modules on practical crypto literacy: how to save money on remittances, how to self-bank securely, and how to avoid scams and fraud.

Day 1 Actions

  • Review curriculum for balance
  • Add practical literacy modules: saving on international remittances, self-custody for unbanked residents, scam recognition and fraud prevention

Program 7

Crypto Tax Payments Low Priority

Adams talked about it, but no actual program exists. If implemented, estimated $850K - $2.45M for Year 1.

Budget Methodology (If Implemented)

Benchmarked against Colorado's program (2022): payment processor integration ($200-600K), DOF system modifications ($150-500K), security/compliance ($100-350K), training ($50-150K), ongoing fees/support ($250-550K). Colorado had <80 total payments in 3 years—cost-benefit is questionable.

Sources: Colorado Crypto Tax, Ohio Program (defunct), Miami MiamiCoin

Problems with Volatile Crypto

  • Capital gains: Paying triggers taxable event
  • Low adoption: Colorado saw <80 payments in 3 years

Stablecoins Solve These

  • No volatility: USDC pegged 1:1 to dollar
  • No capital gains: No appreciation = no tax
  • Already used: Detroit launching with PayPal

Nuanced View

Not inherently bad IF implemented correctly. Require stablecoin support, instant USD conversion, competitive vendor selection. But with <80 payments in 3 years in Colorado, this is low priority.

Program 8

Municipal Bonds Program Reimagine

Re-evaluate with stablecoins (not volatile Bitcoin). Enable lower-income residents to purchase yield-bearing municipal bonds. Use returns for rent control relief.

Budget Methodology

Implementation costs: bond issuance platform ($500K-1.5M), stablecoin integration ($200-500K), low-denomination infrastructure ($300-800K), outreach ($100-300K). Revenue potential: if 100,000 residents invest average $500 at 4% yield, generates $2M annually for rent relief fund.

Sources: NYC Comptroller Debt Mgmt, USDC (Circle), Community Investment

Original BitBonds Problems

  • Bitcoin volatility — 50-80% crashes
  • Federal tax rules prohibit tax-exempt crypto bonds
  • Speculative — city shouldn't run hedge fund

Stablecoin Solution

  • USDC/USDT: No volatility, pegged 1:1 to dollar
  • No capital gains tax for bondholders
  • 24/7 settlement — faster than traditional bonds
  • Lower minimums — accessible to working class

Community Wealth Model

Enable lower-income New Yorkers to invest in city bonds with as little as $25. Yield goes to investors AND funds rent control relief. Builds community stake in city's fiscal health.

Day 1 Actions

  • Feasibility study on stablecoin municipal bonds
  • Partner with community banks for distribution
  • Low-denomination pilot ($25-100 minimum)
  • Rent relief fund allocation from yields
Program 9

NYC Blockchain Plan Pause

Released December 2025 by OTI. We are not convinced this is a good use case for crypto. Asbestos certs, vital records, and property deeds can all be enshrined in publicly verifiable ways without blockchain. Pilot costs TBD.

Budget Methodology

Specific costs not yet published. Based on comparable government blockchain pilots: development ($300K-1M), integration ($500K-1.5M), security audits ($150-400K), per pilot. Dubai Land Registry ($3-5M), Cook County IL property records (~$2M), Estonia e-Residency ($10M+). Each NYC pilot should require cost-benefit analysis.

Sources: NYC OTI, DL News, Estonia e-Residency

Core Skepticism

  • Asbestos Certs — conventional databases can do this
  • Vital Records — non-blockchain public verification exists
  • Property Deeds — existing systems with digital signatures work
  • City Payments — low demand, unnecessary complexity

Salvageable Elements

  • Establishing evaluation criteria is a good direction
  • General approach could work IF community input is added
  • Could improve IF reframed to be NYC-focused, not industry-only

Recommendation: Freeze This Program

Pause all pilots if possible. These use cases do not clearly require blockchain — publicly verifiable data can be achieved through conventional cryptographic methods. If the program continues, require community input, NYC-focused framing, and rigorous alternatives analysis.

Day 1 Actions

  • Freeze all pilots pending review
  • Require alternatives analysis — prove blockchain is necessary
  • If continued: add community input, NYC-focused framing
  • Retain evaluation criteria requirement for any proposals

Proposal 2

Community Pension Funds

Redirect a portion of city pension fund management toward participatory democratic investment using blockchain voting—keeping capital local while maintaining profitability.

Current Problem

Traditional financial firms manage NYC's $250B+ pension funds with minimal public input. Workers' retirement savings often fund gentrification of their own neighborhoods, fossil fuels, and private equity that extracts from local communities.

Algorithmically Enhanced Participatory Budgeting

Using the latest cryptographic identity and algorithmic approaches, there are improvements that can be made at scale for participatory budgeting—such as quadratic voting, which prevents wealthy interests from dominating while amplifying broad community consensus.

  • Quadratic voting: Prevents whale dominance, amplifies broad consensus
  • Delegation: Beneficiaries can delegate votes to trusted community representatives
  • Transparent treasury: All investment decisions recorded for public audit

NYC Example: Harlem District 9 piloted quadratic voting for participatory budgeting in 2023 with RadicalxChange.

Municipalities Already Doing This

  • Moscow Active Citizen: 5M+ users, blockchain-verified voting on city budget priorities since 2017
  • Barcelona Decidim: Open-source participatory democracy platform used by 150+ cities globally
  • Zug, Switzerland: "Crypto Valley" conducted first blockchain municipal vote in 2018

Implementation Path

Start with 1-2% of pension allocation for participatory voting. Use existing tools (Snapshot, Tally) adapted for municipal use. Partner with Gitcoin for quadratic funding expertise.

Proposal 4

Worker-Owned Grocery Co-ops

City-initiated grocery stores run sovereignly by workers through permissionless cryptographic tooling—resilient to future administration changes.

Crypto-Enabled Governance

  • Work-to-vote: Governance weight earned through verified labor hours
  • Work-to-purchase: Ownership stakes earned through participation
  • City cryptographic identity: Verifiable credentials for workers
  • Permissionless tooling: Not dependent on city systems or future administrations

Why This Matters

  • Sovereign worker control: Co-ops run independently by those who work there
  • Resilient to change: Governance lives on-chain, not in city bureaucracy
  • Can't be overwritten: Next administration can't undo worker ownership
  • Decentralized planning: Store decisions made by workers + community, not city

Key Principle

Rather than city-managed stores vulnerable to political change, use crypto to establish permanent worker-owned infrastructure. Governance, participation, and planning facilitated through permissionless cryptographic tooling that will be resilient to future administrations.

Proposal 5

City Currency System

A stablecoin-based municipal payment system that captures value currently extracted by Visa, Mastercard, and other payment processors—redirecting all fees directly into city funding.

Value Being Captured Today

  • 2-3% transaction fees on every card payment in NYC
  • Billions annually flowing to Visa, Mastercard, payment processors
  • Merchant fees passed on as higher prices for residents
  • Zero city benefit from payment infrastructure it enables

Municipal Alternatives Exist

  • Seoul T-money: City-owned payment system (34% municipal ownership), used for transit, retail, and services
  • Lugano, Switzerland: Accepts Bitcoin/USDT for all municipal taxes and services via "Plan B"

Stablecoin Municipal Model

  • NYC Stablecoin: 1:1 USD-backed municipal currency
  • Yield-bearing: Treasury reserves generate interest for city budget
  • Replace credit card rails: Merchants save 2-3% on every transaction
  • Capture all fees: Transaction fees fund city services, not Wall Street
  • Instant settlement: No 3-day wait for merchant deposits

Key Insight

The city already enables billions in commerce through its infrastructure. A municipal stablecoin lets NYC capture the value of its own economy—transaction fees become city revenue, treasury reserves earn yield, and residents benefit from lower merchant costs.
