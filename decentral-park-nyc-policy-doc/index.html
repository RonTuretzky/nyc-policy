Decentralized Infrastructure
Who We Are

What is Decentral Park?

A coalition of technologists, policy advocates, and community organizers working at the intersection of blockchain technology and progressive urban policy.

Our Background

We come from the crypto and blockchain space—not as speculators, but as builders who see these technologies as tools for democratic infrastructure.

Our members include developers, economists, cooperative organizers, and civic technologists who have spent years exploring how decentralized systems can serve public goods.

Our Mission

  • Bridge the gap between crypto innovation and progressive policy
  • Advocate for community-controlled digital infrastructure
  • Support participatory governance experiments
  • Build coalitions with cooperatives, unions, and civic groups
The Thesis

Why Are Progressives Talking About Crypto?

The mainstream narrative around cryptocurrency focuses on speculation and investment. We're interested in something different: infrastructure and participation.

🏛️

The Problem with Centralization

Payment processors extract 2-3% of every transaction. Tech platforms control our data. Financial institutions decide who gets access to capital. These gatekeepers extract value from communities without accountability.

🔓

Crypto as Public Infrastructure

Blockchain technology offers something rare: the ability to build financial and governance infrastructure that no single entity controls. This isn't about replacing government—it's about giving government better tools.

🗳️

Democracy Needs Better Tools

Participatory budgeting, cooperative ownership, transparent public spending—these progressive ideals have been limited by technology. Crypto provides verifiable, tamper-proof systems for democratic participation.

Reframing the Conversation

Crypto as Payment, Not Investment

For most people, "crypto" means Bitcoin prices and NFT speculation. But the underlying technology enables something far more important: public payment and governance infrastructure free from corporate gatekeepers.

What We're NOT Proposing

  • Speculative cryptocurrency investments
  • Replacing the dollar with Bitcoin
  • "Disrupting" government services
  • Deregulation or libertarian policies

What We ARE Proposing

  • City-owned payment rails that don't enrich Visa/Mastercard
  • Transparent, verifiable participatory budgeting
  • Community-controlled identity systems
  • Democratic governance tools that actually work

"NYC is the heart of capitalism but doesn't control capital. These tools can change that."

Proof It Works

Case Study: Quadratic Voting in Harlem

In 2023, RadicalxChange partnered with NYC Council Member Kristin Richardson Jordan to run the first public online Quadratic Voting experiment in US history.

Real World Example District 9 Participatory Budgeting

How It Worked

  • Residents registered at harlemwallet.org
  • Eligibility verified against voter rolls
  • Secure postal verification with QR codes
  • Online voting using Quadratic Voting

Top Funded Projects

  • The Baxter — $1M affordable housing (136 votes)
  • Marcus Garvey Playground — $275K (119 votes)
  • Harlem River Park — $250K (100 votes)

Proposal 02

Participatory Budgeting

Use blockchain-verified voting to give New Yorkers direct say over public investments—from pension funds to capital projects—with transparency and accountability built in.

"NYC is the heart of capitalism but doesn't control capital."

Current Problem

Traditional financial firms manage pension funds with minimal public input, extracting value from NYC communities while investing elsewhere. Participatory budgeting exists but lacks robust verification.

Proposed Solution

  • Set aside fund portion for public voting
  • Focus on NYC businesses, ESG initiatives, cooperatives
  • Decentralized tech for transparency & verifiability
  • Build on RadicalxChange's proven QV model

Proposal 04

City-Owned Grocery Stores

Decentralized governance for public food infrastructure—avoiding "Walmart run by the MTA."

Design Challenges

  • Location strategy (accessibility vs. competitive pressure)
  • Distribution network across large boroughs
  • Co-op model integration
  • Disability access considerations

Proposed Solutions

  • Decentralized governance voting for placement
  • Community-organized delivery shuttles
  • Independent operation per store
  • "City owned bodegas" as alternative model

"Rather than be limited by legal contracts and entities, could be an area that is ripe for crypto to simplify."
